How to Extend a Lease in the UK 5 min read

A short lease can make your property difficult to sell and expensive to mortgage.
Here's how to extend it — and what it costs.

If you own a leasehold property with a lease falling below 80 years, extending it is one of the most important things you can do for its value and mortgageability. Below is a complete guide covering your legal rights, the full process, costs, and what to watch out for.

1. Statutory vs Informal Extension

You have a statutory right under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993 to extend your lease by 90 years (on a flat) at a peppercorn ground rent, provided you have owned the property for at least two years.

Since the Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024, the two-year ownership requirement has been removed for new cases. You can also negotiate an informal extension directly with the freeholder — faster, but with no legal right to a specific term or ground rent.

📌 Key Fact Under the 2024 Act reforms, house lessees can now also extend to 990 years at zero ground rent — a significant improvement from the previous 50-year extension limit.

2. Eligibility Requirements

Who qualifies for a statutory extension?

  1. Leasehold ownership — You must be the registered leaseholder of the property at the Land Registry.
  2. Original lease of 21+ years — The original lease (not remaining term) must have been granted for more than 21 years.
  3. Residential use — The property must be a flat or house used as a residence (not commercial).
  4. No immediate ownership requirement — Since the 2024 Act, you no longer need to have owned for 2 years before serving notice.
✅ Tip If you are purchasing a property with a short lease, your solicitor can assign the existing leaseholder's right to extend — meaning you can serve the Section 42 Notice immediately after completion.

3. How the Premium is Calculated

The premium is calculated using a formal valuation methodology that considers:

  • The current ground rent and future ground rent schedule
  • The remaining lease term at the date of the Section 42 Notice
  • The freehold value and open market value of the property
  • A capitalisation rate (deferment rate, typically ~5%)
⚠️ Critical Threshold — 80 Years Below 80 years, a marriage value element is added to the premium — this is typically the largest cost driver and can add tens of thousands of pounds. Above 80 years, the premium is far more predictable. Act before crossing this threshold.

4. The Step-by-Step Process

  1. Instruct a solicitor experienced in leasehold extensions and a RICS surveyor who specialises in leasehold extension valuations.
  2. RICS Valuation — Your surveyor will advise the appropriate premium range and prepare a formal valuation report to support your offer.
  3. Serve Section 42 Notice — Your solicitor formally serves this notice on the freeholder specifying your proposed premium. This fixes the valuation date.
  4. Freeholder responds — The freeholder has two months to serve a Counter-Notice admitting or denying your right to extend.
  5. Negotiation — The parties (via their surveyors) negotiate the premium. Most cases settle without going to tribunal.
  6. First-tier Tribunal (FTT) — If no agreement is reached within 6 months of the Counter-Notice, either party may apply to the FTT to determine the premium.
  7. Completion — Once agreed, your solicitor drafts and completes the new lease deed. The extension is registered at the Land Registry.
⏱ Timeline The typical end-to-end process takes 6–12 months. Contested cases that go to tribunal can take 18 months or longer. Start early — especially if your lease is approaching 80 years.

6. Impact on Property Value & Mortgageability

Mortgage lenders typically require a minimum of 70–85 years remaining on a lease at the time of application. Many lenders will not lend at all on leases below 70 years.

🚨 Warning A lease below 70 years can reduce your property's value by 10–25% and make it effectively unmortgageable. Do not wait — the marriage value cost of extending will still be far less than the value lost by inaction.

8. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I extend my lease if I have a mortgage?

Yes, but you will need your mortgage lender's consent. Most lenders grant this routinely as the extension improves the security of their loan. Your solicitor will handle the lender's requirements.

What if the freeholder refuses to extend?

If you have served a valid Section 42 Notice and meet the eligibility criteria, the freeholder cannot refuse — they can only dispute the premium. You can apply to the First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber) to have the premium determined.

Can I sell my property while the extension is in progress?

Yes. The benefit of a Section 42 Notice can be assigned to a buyer as part of the conveyancing process — meaning they can continue the extension after purchase.

Does extending the lease affect my service charge?

No — the lease extension only affects the lease term and ground rent. Service charges are set separately by the freeholder or management company under the terms of your existing lease.

💡 Remember You can also negotiate to buy the freehold collectively with other leaseholders in your building (called "collective enfranchisement") — this is often more cost-effective and eliminates ground rent and freeholder management fees entirely.
